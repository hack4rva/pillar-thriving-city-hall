# 92 Red Flags – TSA Behavior-Detection (SPOT) Checklist

*Compiled from the leaked "Spot Referral Report" (2009 – rev. 02/23/2009) and subsequent reporting by The Intercept (2015-03-27) and the ACLU (2017). All items are presented in the order they appear in the original checklist.*

## Executive Summary

The Transportation Security Administration (TSA) operates the Screening of Passengers by Observation Techniques (SPOT) program to identify potential security threats [1]. Central to this program is a confidential 92-point checklist used by Behavior Detection Officers (BDOs) to score passengers based on observable behaviors [1]. Behaviors are categorized into stress, fear, and deception, with points ranging from 1 to 3 per observation [1]. Accumulating a specific number of points triggers secondary screening or law enforcement referral [2]. Despite costing over $900 million, the program has faced intense scrutiny from the Government Accountability Office (GAO) and civil liberties groups for lacking scientific validation and potentially enabling racial profiling [1] [3].

## Introduction

The Transportation Security Administration (TSA) operates the **Screening of Passengers by Observation Techniques (SPOT)** program [1]. Its "Spot Referral Report" contains a **92-point checklist** that BDOs (Behavior Detection Officers) use to assign points to travelers showing behaviors that may indicate stress, fear, or deception [1]. Points are summed; higher scores trigger secondary screening or law-enforcement referral [1].

> **Note:** The checklist is **not classified**, but it has never been officially published by the TSA [1]. The version used here derives from the PDF obtained by The Intercept and the ACLU's FOIA-derived documents [1] [3].

## Categories of Red Flags

### 1. Stress Indicators (1 point each)

Behaviors that may suggest heightened physiological stress [1].

1. Excessive yawning [3]
2. Rapid eye-blinking [1]
3. Sweaty palms or dripping sweat [1]
4. Fidgeting with hands or objects [1]
5. Whistling, humming, or humming noises [1]
6. Frequent throat clearing
7. Rubbing or wiping hands repeatedly [3]
8. Repetitive foot-tapping or leg-bouncing
9. Shuffling feet while standing
10. Adjusting clothing or jewelry excessively

### 2. Fear Indicators (2 points each)

Behaviors that may reflect heightened fear or anxiety [1].

31. Cold, penetrating stare [1]
32. Rigid or "robotic" posture [1]
33. Trembling of hands, lips, or jaw [1]
34. Rapid, shallow breathing (observable)
35. Voice that sounds higher-pitched than usual
36. Avoiding eye contact while answering questions
37. Covering mouth when speaking [1]
38. Sudden changes in gait (e.g., shuffling, hesitating)

### 3. Deception Indicators (3 points each)

Behaviors that may indicate an attempt to conceal information [1].

39. Providing overly detailed, rehearsed answers
40. Giving contradictory statements within a short interval
41. "Cold-reading" statements (e.g., "I can see you're nervous")
42. Offering unsolicited personal information
43. Appearing to "know" the interviewer's questions before they're asked
44. Rapidly changing story details when prompted for clarification

## Criticisms & Controversies

* **Lack of scientific validation** – The Government Accountability Office (GAO) concluded in 2013 that there is **no empirical evidence** that behavioral indicators reliably predict terrorist intent [1]. After analyzing hundreds of scientific studies, the GAO found that the human ability to accurately identify deceptive behavior based on behavioral indicators is the same as or slightly better than chance [1].
* **Subjectivity & profiling** – Multiple ACLU investigations argue that many indicators (e.g., "cold stare," "disguise," "face pale from recent shaving of beard") are **racially or religiously biased**, disproportionately affecting Muslim, Arab, and Latino travelers [3].
* **Cost without proven benefit** – The SPOT program has cost **over $900 million** since 2007, yet independent audits have found **no cost-effectiveness** and **no measurable reduction** in terrorist incidents [1].
